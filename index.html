Climate change and global warming are terms that most people are familiar nowadays. Some of us may discover that summers are getting hotter, winters getting harsher, and extreme events like snow storms and droughts are happening more often. We often read in the news about how ice caps are melting and polar bears are dying. For those who are not familiar with climate change and global warming, we will learn about them briefly in this short study by looking at data, and interpreting data through maps created from ArcMap and visualizations from Tableau.

What is global warming?
Global warming refers to the rise in Earth's atmospheric temperature, which will then lead to a change in climate, including changes in precipitation, which may lead to more floods, droughts, or even heat waves. Moreover, the rising temperature has caused ice caps to melt, putting polar bears at risk and increasing sea levels. Studies have shown that Earth's temperature has risen by about 1.5°F for the past century, and is expected to rise in the future as well. Below is a Tableau visualization that shows the average annual temperature of the US from the year 1905 to 2015. From the upper visualization, we see a trendline that shows us even though there are fluctuations, the overall temperature pattern is indeed increasing. The lower visualization uses different shades of color to show the difference in temperatures. By toggling with the year menu, we will be able to see the variations in temperature in a visual way as opposed to a graphical way like the first map. A darker red signifies a higher temperature and vice versa.

I chose to use a chloropleth map to show carbon dioxide emissions because it could show us which states emit the largest amount of carbon dioxide, which gives us an idea of what areas are possibly more responsible of global warming and should therefore make more efforts in trying to fix the problem. 

In the map, a darker shade of red represents a larger amount of carbon dioxide emitted while a lighter color represents smaller emissions. The numbers in the legend are measured in million metric tons, and I chose natural breaks as the classification method because it showed the contrast between different states the best. As we can see in the map, state like Texas and California have the highest emissions. This chloropleth map however, doesn't clearly show  the exact rankings between the states, nor does it show the difference in emission amount over the years. If we wanted to map out emission of different years using ArcMap, we would have to create several separate maps, which was what I did initially. However, I discovered that these maps take up a lot of space, and are a bit redundant because they essentially show the same map but with minor changes. This is why I decided to use Tableau to create visualizations that allow us to easily see how states compare to each other in terms of CO2 emission amount. The first visualization is a bar chart with a menu that allows you to toggle and see CO2 emissions from 1990 to 2013 all in a single visualization. The second visualization shows a chloropleth map which is very similar to the map created from ArcMap above, but this visualization changes as we toggle the year menu, which again allows us to see CO2 emissions from different years on a single map. The third visualization is a line chart that shows the total amount of CO2 emissions of the United States from 1990 to 2013. Tableau calculates the total national CO2 emission for us by adding up the individual state emissions. Controversy
Although atmospheric temperature has been increasing, some people argue that global warming is not an anomaly, rather it is a natural phenomenon that occurs as a cycle. These people mentioned that the temperature has been rising since two centuries ago and have less to do with human industrial activities. The line chart above in fact shows that carbon dioxide emissions have been decreasing starting in 2007. However, this does not mean carbon dioxide emissions from industrial activities and deforestation would cause no harm to our environment. These emissions cause air pollution and increase green house gases which would warm up our Earth much more than it should. Therefore even if we do not believe in global warming, we have to agree that industrial activities do harm our environment and we should do something to lessen the pressure on our environment. 
Now that we have covered the definitions of global warming and climate changes. Let us see the causes behind this phenomenon. 

Who is behind all of this?
Human activities have contributed greatly to the global warming phenomenon. To obtain energy, people burn fossil fuels which produce a lot of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. Greenhouse gases cause the warming up of the Earth in a process called the greenhouse effect.  These gases act like a blanket that traps the sun's energy, which heats up our Earth's atmosphere abnormally. Another main cause of global warming is deforestation. People cut down trees because of desires of having more land to do industrial practices. However, the felling of trees emit carbon dioxide into the air as well, adding to the severity of the greenhouse effect. Renewable Energy
Rather than burning fossil fuels to obtain energy, another alternative is to use renewable energy which will be much more friendly to our environment. Renewable energy are obtained from natural resources that are naturally replenished, and include wind power, solar energy...etc. In particular, wind energy is a clean fuel source that is sustainable and domestic. Moreover, wind sector projects have increased the number of jobs as well. Shown on the left is a map created from ArcMap that shows all the wind turbine sites in the US in 2015. Moreover, the states are colored in terms of their installed wind capacity, which describes how much electricity or power can be generated by the installed wind turbines in that state. The wind turbine sites are shown as an orange dot. Unfortunately I was not able to find the capacities of each of those wind turbines, otherwise what I wanted to do originally is to represent them on the map differently according to their capacities.
Right now they are all represented in the same way, and since wind turbines may differ in capacity, the abundance of wind turbine symbols in one area will not necessarily mean that wind capacity will be greater in that area. This is also one of the reasons why I wanted to make a chloropleth map in the background to show the installed wind power capacities of the different states. In the legend, the power capacities are measured in megawatts, and a darker shade of purple represents a higher capacity.

Conclusion
No matter global warming is an anomaly or not, atmospheric temperature increasing is a fact and so is industrial activities ruining our environment.
